A full privacy gate delivers the highest level of visual screening available in a PVC gate product. No light or visibility passes through the panel, and the gate provides a clear physical boundary at the property entry. Where homeowners have a choice, the decision usually comes down to one question: sliding gate or swing gate.

Both configurations use the same full privacy PVC panel material. The difference is entirely in how the gate moves when it opens and closes. The right choice depends on your driveway dimensions, site gradient, and how you plan to use the gate.

## Why Full Privacy PVC for a Gate?

PVC full privacy gates use a solid panel construction where boards are pressed together without visible gaps. This design provides maximum visual screening from the street and prevents people on the footpath from seeing into the driveway or front yard.

PVC is particularly suited to Sydney's outdoor conditions. It does not rust, does not warp under moisture, and does not need repainting after sun exposure. UV-stabilised PVC holds its colour through years of direct sunlight, which is relevant in New South Wales where summer UV levels are consistently high.

## The Swing Gate Configuration

A swing gate is the simpler of the two configurations. The panel rotates on hinges around a fixed post. When open, the gate swings to one side, either inward toward the property or outward toward the road. Most residential gates are set to swing inward.

Swing gates suit driveways with enough clearance behind the gate for the full arc of the panel. If a vehicle is parked immediately behind the gate when it is being opened, the gate cannot swing freely. This is the primary limitation of a swing configuration.

For sloped driveways, a swing gate handles moderate gradients well. The panel swings up and over small variations in ground level without requiring a precisely level track.

## The Sliding Gate Configuration

A sliding gate moves horizontally along the fence line. The panel runs on wheels or rollers along a ground track or hangs from an overhead rail. When open, the gate stacks to one side, parallel to the fence.

A sliding gate requires a clear run of fence on the stacking side equal to the gate width. On a six-metre opening, for example, the fence must extend at least six metres on the stacking side to accommodate the open gate. On small blocks, this stacking space is sometimes the deciding factor.

Sliding gates are particularly useful where the driveway cannot accommodate the swing arc of a large gate panel. On steep driveways, a sliding gate can be engineered with a level track even where the surrounding surface is sloped, though this requires careful installation planning.

## The Role of the Driveway Width

The width of the gate opening affects which configuration is practical. Swing gates work well on openings up to approximately four metres. Beyond this width, the combined weight and length of the panel makes a single swing leaf impractical. A double swing gate addresses wider openings but adds hardware complexity.

Sliding gates scale more easily to wider openings because the panel weight is distributed along the track rather than cantilevered from a single hinge point. For driveways wider than four metres, a sliding gate is often more practical and more reliable over time.

## Automation Compatibility

Both gate types are compatible with automation. A swing gate uses an arm actuator attached to the post and the gate panel. A sliding gate uses a chain-driven or rack-and-pinion motor at the base.

If automation is planned now or in the future, discuss this with the supplier at the time of ordering. Gate panels and posts for automated installations may have additional reinforcement or dimension requirements. Installing a gate without this reinforcement and then adding automation later often requires re-installation of the gate and posts.

## Ground and Track Conditions

A sliding gate requires a level or engineered track along the ground. On uneven or sloped ground, the track must be set independently to maintain a true horizontal line. This adds to installation complexity compared to a swing gate.

A swing gate only requires level gate posts. The gate itself will follow a circular arc regardless of minor ground variations, making it more tolerant of irregular surfaces.

## The Decision Framework

Ask yourself these questions before choosing:

- Is the driveway flat with no significant slope? (Favours sliding)
- Is the opening wider than four metres? (Favours sliding)
- Is there adequate fence on one side for the gate to stack when open? (Required for sliding)
- Is the driveway steep or the ground uneven without engineering works? (Favours swing)
- Is the opening narrow or the stacking space limited? (Favours swing)

If the driveway is flat, the opening is wide, and there is adequate fence on one side for stacking, consider a sliding gate. If the driveway is sloped, the opening is narrower, or stacking space is limited, a swing gate is usually the more practical option.

## Summary

Choosing between a sliding gate and a swing gate comes down to your site dimensions and how you plan to use the entry. Both configurations offer the same full privacy performance in PVC. Measure the driveway, assess the available stacking space, and confirm the gradient before making a final decision. The right choice for your site is the one that operates reliably without requiring changes to the driveway layout or ground conditions.
