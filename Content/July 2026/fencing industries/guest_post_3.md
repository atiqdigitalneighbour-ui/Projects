# Bollards and Safety Rails: A Comprehensive Guide to Pedestrian and Property Protection

Vehicle incursions into pedestrian areas are a genuine safety risk at commercial properties, car parks, retail precincts, schools, and public facilities. The consequences of a vehicle entering a pedestrian zone, whether through driver error, mechanical failure, or a deliberate act, can be severe.

Bollards and safety rails provide a physical barrier between vehicle traffic and pedestrian areas. This article explains how they work, where they are most effective, and what to consider when specifying a system for your site.

---

## The Purpose of Bollards

A bollard is a short, sturdy vertical post installed to control or block vehicle access. Bollards serve multiple functions depending on the context:

**Access control.** Bollards can define which vehicles are permitted to access certain areas by physically blocking entry. Retractable or removable bollards allow authorised vehicles to pass while blocking others.

**Vehicle incursion prevention.** At pedestrian entries, shopfronts, footpaths, and outdoor seating areas, fixed bollards prevent vehicles from entering even in the event of driver error or brake failure.

**Perimeter delineation.** Bollards mark the edge of a traffic route, separate a pedestrian path from a vehicle way, or define the boundary of a car park bay without the visual intrusion of a full fence or wall.

**Speed management.** In some applications, bollard spacing is used in combination with other measures to slow vehicle movement through a shared zone.

---

## Types of Bollards

### Fixed Bollards

Fixed bollards are permanently anchored in concrete. They provide the highest level of protection against vehicle incursion because they cannot be moved or bypassed without significant force. Fixed bollards are appropriate where permanent access restriction is required.

### Removable Bollards

Removable bollards are anchored in a receiver set into the ground. They can be lifted and stored to allow access and reinstalled to restrict it. This is suitable for areas that require access at certain times and restricted access at others.

### Retractable Bollards

Retractable bollards are hydraulically or electrically operated and can be raised or lowered remotely or via a keypad. They are used in higher-security or higher-traffic applications where frequent operation is needed.

### Surface-Mounted Bollards

Where excavation is not possible, surface-mounted bollards are anchored to the existing surface with high-strength fixings. They offer less resistance to vehicle impact than excavated bollards but are a practical option in certain situations.

---

## Safety Rails

Safety rails serve a similar protective function to bollards but cover a linear distance rather than individual points. They are commonly used:

- along the edge of elevated platforms, loading docks, and mezzanine levels
- alongside pedestrian walkways adjacent to vehicle routes
- at car park perimeters to prevent vehicles from falling from elevated decks
- inside warehouses and industrial buildings to protect racking, columns, and pedestrian routes from forklift and vehicle impact

Safety rails must be designed to absorb a specified impact force without failure. The required impact rating depends on the type of vehicle expected to apply force and the consequence of rail failure.

---

## Specification Considerations

### Vehicle Impact Rating

The impact rating of a bollard or safety rail system determines how much vehicle force it can absorb without failure. For most commercial applications, a standard fixed bollard or heavy-duty safety rail provides adequate protection. High-risk targets may require products tested and rated to recognised standards.

### Height and Visibility

The height of a bollard affects its effectiveness as a visual deterrent and its resistance to vehicle impact. A bollard that is too low may pass under the bumper of a large vehicle without stopping it. The height must be matched to the vehicle types expected in the area.

Reflective strips, bright colours, or illuminated bollard caps improve visibility in low-light conditions.

### Spacing

The spacing between bollards must be calculated to prevent vehicle access whilst allowing pedestrian and wheelchair passage. Typical spacing for pedestrian access is between 900mm and 1,200mm, which allows wheelchair access but prevents standard vehicles from passing through.

### Surface Finish

Bollards are available in a range of finishes including raw steel, galvanised steel, powder-coated steel, stainless steel, and polyethylene sleeved. The finish selection should reflect the environment, the maintenance schedule, and the aesthetic requirements.

## Planning Your Installation

An effective installation begins with a risk assessment. This assessment should identify:

- the types of vehicles operating in and around the area
- the pedestrian movements and peak traffic times
- the existing access control measures in place
- the specific zones where vehicle incursion is a realistic risk
- any regulatory or consent requirements applicable to the site

## Common Applications

**Retail and hospitality frontages.** Protecting outdoor dining areas, shopfront glass, and pedestrian entry zones from errant vehicles.

**Schools and childcare facilities.** Separating drop-off and pick-up vehicle traffic from pedestrian zones where children are present.

**Car parks.** Marking bay edges, protecting structural columns, and preventing vehicles from driving over footpaths.

**Industrial and logistics sites.** Protecting warehouse racking, pedestrian walkways, and plant and equipment from forklift and vehicle impact.

**Public spaces.** Markets, event areas, and public plazas increasingly use bollards to prevent vehicle incursion into crowded pedestrian areas.
